# kid-content — translate human knowledge for kids, at Apple quality

This skill exists because our job is to **teach real knowledge to real kids** — not
to hand them a guessing game or hide ideas behind baby words. Every kid-facing word
and pixel gets worked by a panel of specialists until it is accurate, clear, warm,
and genuinely fun.

## Our focus group (know them cold)

- **Age 9–13** — tweens / early teens. Bright, curious, and they detect
  condescension instantly. Talk *up* to them, not down.
- They go to a **German-language school** and meet our app **in English** (immersion).
  So: ESL-friendly vocabulary, short clean sentences, define every new word the moment
  it appears.
- They live in **Minecraft, Harry Potter, Star Wars, YouTube, games and memes.** Use
  framings from their world when it makes an idea click.
- They are motivated by **competence and autonomy**: "I figured it out myself" beats
  "the app told me." Reward effort; **never shame a wrong answer.**

## Non-negotiable principles

1. **Real words, explained.** Use the adult term (*inert, valence, electron, atomic
   number, perimeter…*) AND define it in one plain sentence with an example. Never
   swap in a vague substitute and never silently omit it.
2. **Always reveal the source.** Don't assert "Oxygen has 2 arms." Show *where it
   comes from* so the kid can work it out for ANY case (e.g. atomic number → electrons
   → shells → what's missing). Teach the method, not the answer.
3. **One new idea at a time.** Concrete before abstract. Show, don't tell. Build on
   what they already know.
4. **Earn every word.** If a sentence doesn't add understanding or delight, cut it.
5. **Curiosity over completeness.** Leave them feeling smart and wanting the next bit.

## The specialist panel (run the draft through every lens)

Adopt each role in turn and note what *that specialist* would change. For a big piece,
you may fan these out as subagents; for routine copy, apply them inline. Capture the
concrete edits, not vague praise.

1. **Subject-matter expert** (scientist / mathematician) — Is it *true*? Flag any
   false or misleading simplification. A kid-friendly version must still be correct.
2. **Learning designer / pedagogue** — Prerequisites met? Is the *source* of each
   rule given? One concept at a time? Right scaffolding and progression?
3. **Child & adolescent psychologist** — Age-appropriate cognitive load? Supports
   autonomy/competence? Encourages, never shames? Tone right for a tween?
4. **Children's author / storyteller** — Where's the wonder, the voice, the metaphor,
   the tiny thread of story? Make it memorable, not a textbook.
5. **Children's-literature editor** — Reading level, rhythm, concision. Cut filler,
   fix clunky lines, keep sentences short.
6. **Kids' science communicator / journalist** — Vivid, *accurate* analogies; the
   "whoa" hook; a real-world connection. (Think the best kids' explainers.)
7. **Plain-language & accessibility editor** — ESL-friendly words; every new term
   defined inline; for UI: contrast (AA), visible focus, reduced-motion, nothing cut off.
8. **UX writer / product designer** (interfaces) — Apple-level microcopy; obvious
   action; tap targets ≥44px (these are kids); delightful but uncluttered; labels and
   empty/error states that teach.
9. **The 11-year-old** (focus-group voice, has veto) — Is it *fun*? cool or cringe?
   "Do I get it? would I keep going?" Kills boredom and talking-down on sight.

## Find a fresh framing

Actively look for a **modern, original, elegant metaphor** the audience loves —
crafting slots / LEGO studs / inventory / a game mechanic / a Star Wars or Minecraft
idea — and **replace a tired metaphor if a better one exists**. (Example under debate:
bonding "arms" for valence — maybe "connectors", "LEGO studs", or a crafting metaphor
lands better. Test it against the panel and the 11-year-old.)

## Process

1. Restate the idea in one true sentence (the expert's version).
2. Run the panel; collect concrete edits + accuracy flags + framing options.
3. Synthesize into the refined text / UI copy.
4. Return three things: **(a)** the final copy, **(b)** a short note of the key changes
   and any accuracy flags, **(c)** the chosen framing/metaphor and why.
5. Iterate if any lens (especially the kid and the expert) isn't satisfied.

## The bar

Apple-level: accurate, warm, every word earning its place, sparking curiosity, and a
kid finishes feeling *smart* and wanting more. If it reads like a textbook or a baby
book, it isn't done.

## When to run

Every lesson, explanation, intro, discovery card, button label, hint, and
error/empty state — and every new UI screen — **before it ships.**
